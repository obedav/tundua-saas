Canada offers some of the most affordable pathways to international education — but its system works differently from the UK. There is no “CAS” and no standardised deposit. Instead, each university sets its own deposit policy, and your Letter of Acceptance (LOA) from a Designated Learning Institution (DLI) is what you need for your study permit. This guide shows you which Canadian universities cost the least upfront in 2026.

For Nigerian students comparing the UK and Canada, the biggest differences are: Canada’s tuition can be significantly lower at specific universities, the post-graduation work permit (PGWP) lasts up to 3 years (vs 2 in the UK), and Canada offers a clearer pathway to permanent residency. The trade-off is that average tuition across all Canadian universities (CAD $41,746/year) is actually higher than average UK tuition — so choosing the right university matters enormously.

How University Deposits and the LOA Work in Canada

Unlike the UK, where every university issues a standardised CAS after deposit payment, Canada’s process varies by institution:

1

Apply and Receive an Offer

Apply directly to a Designated Learning Institution (DLI). If accepted, you receive a conditional or unconditional offer letter.

2

Pay a Tuition Deposit (CAD $500–$5,000)

Most universities require a tuition deposit to confirm your acceptance and issue the official Letter of Acceptance (LOA). Deposit amounts vary widely — some are as low as CAD $500, others require full first-semester tuition upfront. The deposit is deducted from your first tuition payment.

3

Receive Your Letter of Acceptance (LOA)

The LOA is your key document for the study permit application — equivalent to the UK’s CAS. It contains your student ID, programme details, and confirms the DLI has accepted you.

4

Apply for a Study Permit

Submit your study permit application online with your LOA, proof of funds, passport, and other documents. The study permit allows you to study and work in Canada.

💡
DLI status is critical Only Designated Learning Institutions (DLIs) can issue LOAs that are valid for study permit applications. Always verify your university’s DLI status on the Immigration, Refugees and Citizenship Canada (IRCC) website before paying any deposit.

Proof of funds requirement: You must show evidence of at least CAD $22,895/year for living expenses (as of September 2025), plus your first year’s tuition. For example, if tuition is CAD $20,000, you need to show access to CAD $20,000 + $22,895 = CAD $42,895 (~₦50,000,000 at current rates).

⚠️
GIC may be required for Nigerian applicants Some Nigerian students are required to purchase a Guaranteed Investment Certificate (GIC) of CAD $20,635 from a participating Canadian bank as part of their proof of funds. The GIC funds are released to you in monthly instalments after you arrive in Canada. Check the current requirements for your specific situation on the IRCC website.

How to Reduce Your Costs in Canada

  • Co-op programmes: Universities like SFU, Waterloo, and UBC offer co-operative education where you alternate between study and paid work terms. Co-op earnings of CAD $15,000–$25,000 per work term can significantly offset tuition.
  • Work on campus and off campus: Study permit holders can work up to 20 hours/week during term and full-time during scheduled breaks. At CAD $15–$17/hour (minimum wage varies by province), this adds CAD $1,200–$1,400/month.
  • Target provinces with fast PR pathways: Manitoba (MPNP), Saskatchewan (SINP), New Brunswick (NBPNP), and Atlantic provinces have Provincial Nominee Programs specifically for international graduates. PR means you switch to domestic tuition rates if you continue studying, and opens the door to Canadian citizenship.
  • Apply for scholarships: Many Canadian universities offer entrance scholarships for international students (CAD $2,000–$10,000). The Vanier Canada Graduate Scholarship covers CAD $50,000/year for PhD students. Apply early — most deadlines are 6–9 months before intake.
  • Choose smaller cities: Brandon, St. John’s, Moncton, and Saskatoon have the lowest living costs in Canada. Rent alone can be CAD $500–$800/month vs CAD $1,500–$2,500 in Toronto or Vancouver.
